Literature Review Synthesis Guide

From Article Summaries to Scholarly Synthesis

A Guide for Doctoral Students Writing Chapter 2

üéØ THE PROBLEM

Most doctoral students begin Chapter 2 by writing article summaries. This approach fails to demonstrate scholarly thinking and creates a weak foundation for the dissertation.

‚ùå What Article Summaries Look Like (DON'T DO THIS):

> Smith (2020) studied teacher retention in urban schools. He surveyed 200 teachers and found that mentoring programs improved retention rates by 15%. His study used quantitative methods and was conducted in Texas.

>

> Jones (2019) also studied retention. She interviewed 30 teachers about why they stay or leave. She found that salary was important. Her study was qualitative and took place in California.

>

> Williams (2021) looked at working conditions and teacher retention. He found that class size and resources matter. He surveyed 150 teachers in rural schools.

Problems with this approach:

  • Reads like an annotated bibliography
  • No connection between studies
  • No analysis or critique
  • No patterns identified
  • Doesn't build toward YOUR study
  • Passive voice (studies "do" things)
  • Boring to read

‚úÖ What Synthesis Looks Like (DO THIS INSTEAD):

> Teacher retention in high-poverty schools is influenced by multiple interconnected factors operating at individual, organizational, and systemic levels. While compensation serves as a necessary threshold (Jones, 2019; Williams, 2021), research consistently identifies supportive leadership and meaningful mentoring as more decisive retention factors (Smith, 2020; Davis, 2019).

>

> Notably, the relative importance of these factors varies by career stage and context. Early-career teachers prioritize mentoring relationships and professional growth opportunities (Smith, 2020), while mid-career teachers emphasize autonomy and pathways to leadership (Williams, 2021). This developmental pattern suggests retention interventions should be differentiated rather than uniform.

>

> However, most retention research suffers from methodological limitations. Quantitative studies dominate the literature (15 of 20 studies reviewed), capturing breadth but lacking depth on the mechanisms through which factors influence decisions. The few qualitative studies (Chen, 2019; Williams, 2021) provide rich description but limited generalizability. No published research has employed mixed methods to examine both patterns and processes simultaneously‚Äîa gap this dissertation addresses.

Why this is better:

  • YOUR voice leads the narrative
  • Multiple sources integrated per claim
  • Patterns and contradictions identified
  • Critical analysis of methods
  • Builds logically toward your study
  • Gap clearly identified
  • Engaging to read

üìö FOUR SYNTHESIS STRATEGIES

Strategy 1: Thematic Organization

Instead of: Organizing by author or chronology

Do: Organize by themes that cut across studies

Example Structure:

Chapter 2: Literature Review

Section 2.1: Individual Factors Influencing Retention

  • Personal characteristics
  • Motivation and commitment
  • Preparation and training
  • [Synthesize across 10-15 studies]

Section 2.2: Organizational Factors

  • Leadership and administrative support
  • School culture and climate
  • Resources and working conditions
  • [Synthesize across 10-15 studies]

Section 2.3: Systemic and Policy Factors

  • Compensation and benefits
  • Accountability policies
  • Labor market conditions
  • [Synthesize across 8-12 studies]

Section 2.4: Interactions and Complexity

  • How factors interact
  • Mediation and moderation
  • Context matters
  • [Synthesize across 5-8 studies]

Section 2.5: Gaps and Methodological Limitations

  • What we don't know
  • How we could know better
  • Why it matters
  • [Critical analysis leading to your study]

Within Each Theme Section:

Opening paragraph: Introduce the theme and its importance

Body paragraphs: Synthesize findings

  • What do most studies agree on? (convergent findings)
  • Where do studies disagree? (divergent findings)
  • Why might they disagree? (methodological, contextual differences)

Closing paragraph: Transition to next theme or identify gaps

Strategy 2: Identify and Discuss Patterns

Synthesis requires identifying patterns across studies, not just reporting individual findings.

Types of Patterns to Look For:

A. Convergent Findings (Agreement)

What do most studies consistently find?

Example:

> Leadership support emerges as the most consistent predictor of teacher retention across contexts. Of the 18 studies examining administrative factors, 16 found significant positive effects (Chen, 2019; Davis, 2021; Smith, 2020; Williams, 2021; [12 more citations]). This convergence spans urban, suburban, and rural contexts, suggesting leadership support functions as a universal retention factor rather than a context-specific one.

Language to use:

  • Consistently, convergently, uniformly
  • Across studies, throughout the literature
  • Regardless of context, spanning contexts
  • The preponderance of evidence suggests...

B. Divergent Findings (Disagreement)

Where do studies contradict each other? Why?

Example:

> Findings diverge regarding the relative importance of salary versus working conditions. Survey research emphasizes compensation (Jones, 2019; Miller, 2020), while interview-based studies highlight working conditions (Chen, 2019; Williams, 2021). This divergence may reflect methodological artifacts: forced-choice survey items privilege easily quantified factors like salary, while open-ended interviews reveal harder-to-measure but potentially more influential factors like administrative support and collegial relationships.

Language to use:

  • However, in contrast, conversely
  • Findings diverge, results conflict
  • Studies disagree on...
  • This inconsistency may reflect...
  • Possible explanations for this divergence include...

C. Gaps in Knowledge

What hasn't been studied? What questions remain unanswered?

Example:

> Despite substantial research on individual retention factors, few studies examine their interactions. Do supportive administrators compensate for low salaries, or are both necessary? Does the effect of mentoring depend on school culture? These interaction effects remain largely unexplored, limiting our understanding of retention as a complex, systemic phenomenon. This dissertation addresses this gap by examining how individual, organizational, and policy factors interact to influence retention decisions.

Language to use:

  • Gap, absence, lack of research
  • Few studies have examined...
  • Remains unclear, unexplored
  • Little is known about...
  • No research has addressed...

D. Methodological Patterns

What approaches dominate? What's missing methodologically?

Example:

> The retention literature is dominated by cross-sectional survey research (22 of 30 studies reviewed), providing snapshots but not trajectories. Only four longitudinal studies track teachers over time (Davis, 2021; Smith, 2020), and none employ repeated qualitative data collection to understand how retention decisions evolve. This methodological gap limits understanding of retention as a developmental process rather than a one-time decision.

Language to use:

  • Methodologically, the literature...
  • Most studies employ...
  • Few studies use...
  • Absent from the literature are...
  • This methodological limitation means...

Strategy 3: Build Toward Your Study

Every section of Chapter 2 should progressively build the argument for WHY your study is needed.

The Building Blocks Structure:

1. What We Know (Establish the Foundation)

Start each major section by synthesizing current knowledge. Show you understand the field.

Example:

> Research establishes that teacher retention is multiply determined. Individual factors (motivation, preparation), organizational factors (leadership, culture), and systemic factors (policy, labor markets) all contribute (comprehensive synthesis with 20+ citations). This multifaceted understanding represents significant progress from early single-factor models (historical citation).

2. What We Don't Know (Identify the Gap)

Transition from what's established to what's missing.

Example:

> However, three critical gaps limit current understanding. First, most research treats factors independently, failing to examine interactions. Second, quantitative dominance means mechanisms remain poorly understood. Third, virtually no research considers how retention factors vary across different educational contexts.

3. Why It Matters (Establish Significance)

Explain the consequences of the gap‚Äîwhy does it matter that we don't know this?

Example:

> These gaps have practical consequences. Districts invest millions in uniform retention initiatives without understanding how factors interact or vary by context. Money is wasted, teachers continue leaving, and students in high-need schools suffer from chronic instability. Better theoretical understanding could enable more targeted, effective, and efficient retention strategies.

4. How Your Study Addresses the Gap (Your Contribution)

Show how your study specifically addresses what's missing.

Example:

> This dissertation addresses these gaps through a mixed-methods multilevel study that: (1) examines interactions among retention factors using hierarchical linear modeling, (2) explores mechanisms through teacher interviews and administrator case studies, and (3) compares patterns across urban, suburban, and rural contexts. This design provides both breadth and depth, revealing patterns and processes that neither quantitative nor qualitative approaches alone could capture.

Strategy 4: Use Transitional Synthesis Paragraphs

Between major sections, write synthesis paragraphs that:

  • Summarize what was just covered
  • Identify tensions or unresolved questions
  • Bridge to the next section
  • Maintain narrative flow

Example Transition (End of "Individual Factors" section, moving to "Organizational Factors"):

> The preceding review establishes that individual characteristics‚Äîmotivation, preparation, commitment‚Äîmatter for retention. However, individual factors cannot fully explain retention patterns. Teachers with similar backgrounds and motivations experience vastly different retention rates across schools (Smith, 2020), suggesting organizational contexts shape retention independent of individual characteristics. The following section examines how school-level factors influence retention, both directly and through interaction with individual variables.

What this does:

  • Summarizes previous section (individual factors matter)
  • Identifies limitation (can't explain variation across schools)
  • Provides evidence for transition (same teachers, different outcomes)
  • Previews next section (organizational factors)
  • Shows relationship between sections (interaction, not just addition)

‚úçÔ∏è SYNTHESIS WRITING TECHNIQUES

Technique 1: Cluster Citations

Instead of citing sources one at a time:

> Smith (2020) found X. Jones (2019) also found X. Davis (2021) found X too.

Cluster related findings:

> Research consistently demonstrates X (Davis, 2021; Jones, 2019; Smith, 2020).

Benefits:

  • More concise
  • Shows convergence at a glance
  • Keeps focus on YOUR argument, not individual studies
  • Allows efficient use of space

When to cite individually:

  • When making specific methodological points
  • When one study is particularly important
  • When showing disagreement between specific studies

Technique 2: Use Comparison and Contrast Language

Show relationships between studies explicitly:

Agreement:

  • Similarly, likewise, in agreement
  • Consistent with, corroborating, supporting
  • Also, additionally, moreover
  • Convergent with, aligning with

Disagreement:

  • However, in contrast, conversely
  • Despite, although, whereas
  • Diverging from, contradicting, challenging
  • Alternatively, on the other hand

Extension:

  • Building on, extending, expanding
  • Going beyond, advancing
  • Adding to, complementing

Example of effective use:

> Leadership support predicts retention in urban schools (Smith, 2020). Similarly, suburban districts with strong administrative support show higher retention (Jones, 2019). However, the specific leadership practices that matter differ by context: urban teachers value advocacy and resource acquisition (Smith, 2020), while suburban teachers prioritize instructional feedback (Jones, 2019). This pattern suggests leadership support is universally important but contextually enacted.

Technique 3: Critical Analysis, Not Just Description

Don't just report what studies found‚Äîanalyze and critique them.

Methodological Critique

Weak (just describing):

> Smith (2020) used surveys to study retention.

Strong (critiquing):

> While Smith's (2020) large-scale survey provides valuable breadth (n=2,000), the cross-sectional design cannot establish temporal precedence. Do supportive principals retain teachers, or do stable teaching staffs allow principals to be more supportive? Longitudinal designs are needed to untangle causality.

Theoretical Critique

Weak:

> Jones (2019) used social capital theory.

Strong:

> Jones (2019) applies social capital theory to explain how professional networks influence retention. However, Bourdieu's framework emphasizes power differentials in network access‚Äîa dimension Jones overlooks. Teachers in different positions (novice vs. veteran, marginalized vs. privileged) have unequal access to supportive networks, potentially explaining retention disparities Jones attributes to "networking" without examining structural barriers.

Gap Identification

Weak:

> No one has studied X.

Strong:

> Despite extensive research on retention factors, the question of why certain factors matter more in some contexts than others remains unexplored. Existing research identifies what predicts retention but not how or why those predictors operate differently across settings. Understanding mechanisms‚Äînot just correlations‚Äîis essential for developing transferable retention strategies rather than context-specific, atheoretical interventions.

Technique 4: Your Voice Leads the Narrative

YOU are the author of Chapter 2. Sources support YOUR argument; they don't make arguments for you.

Weak (sources lead):

> Smith (2020) says that mentoring improves retention. Jones (2019) found that salary is important. Davis (2021) discovered that working conditions matter.

Strong (you lead):

> Teacher retention is shaped by multiple reinforcing factors. The literature converges on three primary influences: supportive mentoring relationships, adequate compensation, and positive working conditions (Davis, 2021; Jones, 2019; Smith, 2020). Of these, mentoring emerges as most decisive in studies that compare relative effect sizes (Smith, 2020), though adequate compensation serves as a necessary threshold without which other factors cannot compensate (Jones, 2019).

What makes this strong:

  • YOU make the claim (retention is multiply determined)
  • YOU identify the pattern (three primary influences)
  • YOU interpret the hierarchy (mentoring most decisive)
  • Sources provide evidence for YOUR analysis

üîç EXAMPLES: BEFORE AND AFTER

Example 1: Principal Leadership and Teacher Retention

‚ùå Before (Summary Approach):

> Smith (2020) studied principal leadership and teacher retention. She surveyed 500 teachers in urban districts. She found that instructional leadership was positively correlated with retention (r = .45, p < .001). Her study used regression analysis.

>

> Jones (2019) also looked at leadership. He interviewed 30 teachers who left their schools. He found that lack of principal support was a major reason for leaving. His study was qualitative.

>

> Davis (2021) examined transformational leadership. She found that principals who inspire and empower teachers have higher retention rates. Her study included 75 schools.

Problems:

  • Three separate paragraphs for three studies
  • No connections made
  • Just reporting what each author did
  • No synthesis or analysis
  • No critique
  • No connection to your study

‚úÖ After (Synthesis Approach):

> Principal leadership emerges as a critical retention factor, though the specific leadership dimensions that matter remain contested. Quantitative research emphasizes instructional leadership‚Äîsetting academic direction, observing classrooms, providing feedback‚Äîas the strongest predictor of retention (r = .45 in Smith's 2020 urban study; Œ≤ = .38 in Davis's 2021 multi-site analysis). However, qualitative research reveals that emotional and advocacy dimensions may be equally important. Jones's (2019) exit interviews found that departing teachers rarely cited lack of instructional feedback; instead, they described feeling unsupported, unprotected, and unvalued‚Äîrelational rather than technical concerns.

>

> This divergence likely reflects methodological artifacts. Survey instruments operationalize leadership through observable behaviors (classroom visits, feedback sessions), while interviews capture experiential dimensions harder to quantify (feeling supported, sensing trust). The implication is troubling: if retention interventions target only the instructional behaviors measured in surveys, they may miss relational dimensions that teachers themselves identify as decisive. This dissertation addresses this gap through a mixed-methods design examining both quantitative patterns and qualitative experiences of principal support.

Why this is better:

  • ONE integrated synthesis paragraph (not three separate summaries)
  • Pattern identified (quantitative vs. qualitative findings diverge)
  • Critical analysis (why do they diverge? measurement issues)
  • Implications discussed (what does this mean for practice?)
  • Gap identified and connected to your study
  • YOUR voice throughout (not letting studies speak for themselves)

Example 2: Teacher Preparation and Retention

‚ùå Before:

> Williams (2021) studied teacher preparation programs. She compared traditionally prepared teachers with alternatively certified teachers. She found that traditionally prepared teachers stayed longer. Her sample included 1,200 teachers.

>

> Chen (2019) looked at preparation quality. She found that teachers from high-quality programs stayed at higher rates. She used state data.

>

> Miller (2020) examined subject-specific preparation. Math and science teachers need strong content knowledge. His study showed that teachers with deeper content preparation had better retention.

‚úÖ After:

> Teacher preparation quality influences early-career retention, though the mechanisms remain poorly understood. Teachers from traditional certification programs show 15-20% higher five-year retention rates than alternatively certified peers (Chen, 2019; Williams, 2021), and within traditional programs, graduates of higher-rated institutions stay at higher rates (Chen, 2019). These patterns persist even after controlling for salary and working conditions, suggesting preparation itself‚Äînot just the characteristics it correlates with‚Äîmatters.

>

> However, "quality" remains a black box. Is it content knowledge (Miller, 2020)? Pedagogical skill? Realistic expectations developed during student teaching? Socialization into the profession? Quantitative studies establish that preparation matters but cannot identify the active ingredients. Without understanding mechanisms, preparation reform proceeds through faith rather than evidence.

>

> This dissertation opens the black box by combining preparation quality metrics with interviews exploring how different preparation experiences shape teachers' sense of readiness, professional identity, and commitment. This design connects patterns to processes, revealing not just that preparation matters but why and how.

Why this is better:

  • Synthesis across studies shows convergent pattern
  • Critical analysis identifies what's missing (mechanisms)
  • Uses metaphor effectively ("black box")
  • Questions are posed that research hasn't answered
  • Clear connection to your study's contribution

üìã CHAPTER 2 SELF-ASSESSMENT CHECKLIST

Use this checklist to evaluate your literature review draft:

Structure and Organization

  • [ ] Thematic organization (not author-by-author or chronological)
  • [ ] Clear structure visible without headings (logical flow)
  • [ ] Each section has clear purpose in building toward your study
  • [ ] Transitions between sections explain relationships and maintain flow
  • [ ] Progressive narrowing (broad to specific, general to your study)

Synthesis Quality

  • [ ] Synthesis across studies (not summary of individual studies)
  • [ ] Patterns identified (convergence, divergence, gaps)
  • [ ] Multiple sources per claim (clustered citations)
  • [ ] Contradictions noted and explained (not ignored)
  • [ ] Your voice leads (sources support YOUR argument)

Critical Analysis

  • [ ] Methodological critique (what works, what's limited)
  • [ ] Theoretical critique (frameworks evaluated)
  • [ ] Gap identification (what's missing and why it matters)
  • [ ] Comparison across studies (not just description)
  • [ ] Implications discussed (so what? why does this matter?)

Connection to Your Study

  • [ ] Gap clearly identified (what we don't know)
  • [ ] Significance established (why it matters that we don't know)
  • [ ] Your study addresses gap (explicit connection)
  • [ ] Builds logically toward Chapter 3 (methods follow from gaps)

Writing Quality

  • [ ] Active voice predominates (you lead, not sources)
  • [ ] Clear topic sentences (main idea up front)
  • [ ] Comparison/contrast language (shows relationships)
  • [ ] Concise (no unnecessary repetition)
  • [ ] Engaging to read (not boring list of studies)

Technical Quality

  • [ ] APA format correct (citations and references)
  • [ ] No orphan citations (every cited source in references)
  • [ ] No ghost references (every reference was cited)
  • [ ] Accurate representation (you read the sources, not just abstracts)
  • [ ] Current literature (mostly last 5-10 years, with seminal older works)

üéØ COMMON MISTAKES AND HOW TO FIX THEM

Mistake 1: The Annotated Bibliography

Problem:

> Smith (2020) studied X and found Y.

> Jones (2019) studied X and found Z.

> Davis (2021) studied X and found Y also.

Fix: Cluster related findings

> Research converges on Y (Davis, 2021; Smith, 2020), though Jones (2019) found Z, possibly due to [methodological or contextual difference].

Mistake 2: The Literature Dump

Problem:

Listing 40 studies in one paragraph with no analysis:

> Many researchers have studied retention (Author1, 2020; Author2, 2019; Author3, 2021; Author4, 2020; Author5, 2019;... [35 more citations]).

Fix: Synthesize with selective citation

> Extensive research on retention (see Smith, 2020 for review) identifies three primary factors: [factors]. Of these, [factor] shows the strongest and most consistent effects across contexts (meta-analysis by Jones, 2019).

Mistake 3: Ignoring Contradictions

Problem:

Only citing studies that agree with your hypothesis or each other.

Fix: Acknowledge and explain divergence

> While most research finds X (10 studies), three studies report Y (citations). This divergence may reflect [differences in samples, measures, contexts, or methods]. Resolving this contradiction requires [what your study or future research might do].

Mistake 4: Missing the "So What?"

Problem:

Reporting findings without discussing implications or importance.

Fix: Always address "so what?"

> Research shows X (citations). This matters because [practical implication]. However, we still don't know [gap], which limits [consequence]. Understanding [gap] could enable [practical benefit].

Mistake 5: Weak Transitions

Problem:

Abrupt jumps between topics with no connection.

Fix: Write transitional synthesis paragraphs

> The preceding section established [summary]. However, [tension or question]. The following section addresses [next topic], showing how [relationship to previous section].

üìù SYNTHESIS WRITING TEMPLATES

Template 1: Convergent Findings

Use when most studies agree:

> [Claim]. [Support from multiple studies with clustered citations]. This pattern holds across [contexts], suggesting [interpretation].

Example:

> Supportive leadership predicts retention. Positive administrator relationships increase five-year retention rates by 15-30% across urban, suburban, and rural contexts (Chen, 2019; Davis, 2021; Jones, 2020; Smith, 2019). This consistency suggests leadership support functions as a universal retention factor rather than a context-specific influence.

Template 2: Divergent Findings

Use when studies contradict:

> While [some studies] find X, [other studies] find Y. This divergence may reflect [possible explanation: methodological, contextual, or theoretical differences]. [Implications of the disagreement]. [How your study might resolve it OR what future research is needed].

Example:

> While quantitative studies emphasize salary as the primary retention factor (Jones, 2020; Miller, 2019), qualitative research reveals working conditions and support as more decisive (Chen, 2021; Williams, 2020). This divergence may reflect survey response bias‚Äîteachers uncomfortable admitting financial motivation on surveys but candid in confidential interviews. The implication is that retention incentives focusing solely on salary increases may miss teachers' actual priorities. Mixed-methods research could clarify whether stated preferences (working conditions) or revealed preferences (salary response) better predict actual behavior.

Template 3: Gap Identification

Use when something is missing from literature:

> Despite extensive research on [topic], [specific gap] remains unexplored. [What we don't know]. This gap matters because [consequences or limitations]. This dissertation addresses the gap by [your approach].

Example:

> Despite extensive research on individual retention factors, their interactions remain unexplored. Do supportive principals compensate for low salaries? Does mentoring effectiveness depend on school culture? Is leadership support more important for novices or veterans? This gap matters because interventions targeting single factors may fail if effects depend on presence of other factors. This dissertation addresses the gap by examining two-way and three-way interactions using multilevel modeling, revealing which factor combinations matter most for retention.

Template 4: Methodological Critique

Use when critiquing research approaches:

> While [studies] provide [strength], [limitation] constrains interpretation. [Specific methodological issue]. The consequence is [what we can't conclude]. Addressing this requires [methodological need that your study might meet].

Example:

> While cross-sectional surveys provide breadth (Smith's 2020 study included 2,000 teachers), causal inference is impossible without temporal precedence. Do supportive principals retain teachers, or do stable staffs make principals appear supportive? The consequence is that we cannot distinguish cause from correlation, limiting confidence in intervention recommendations. Addressing this requires longitudinal designs tracking teachers and administrators over time‚Äîa feature of this dissertation's three-year panel study.

üèÜ EXEMPLAR PARAGRAPH: PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

Here's a paragraph that demonstrates excellent synthesis:

> Teacher retention in high-need schools represents a persistent challenge with well-documented consequences. High-poverty schools experience 50% higher turnover than affluent schools (Smith, 2020), disrupting instructional continuity, depleting social capital, and perpetuating achievement gaps (Jones, 2019; Williams, 2021). While retention factors are well-researched‚Äîadministrator support, competitive compensation, manageable working conditions, and meaningful mentoring consistently predict retention (meta-analysis by Chen, 2019)‚Äîimplementation research remains sparse. We know what matters but not how to deliver it effectively in under-resourced contexts where these supports are least available but most needed. Affluent districts with resources to provide comprehensive support show strong retention (Davis, 2021), but high-need districts struggle to afford such programs (Miller, 2020). This creates a cruel paradox: schools most needing retention interventions lack capacity to implement evidence-based practices, while well-resourced schools already providing such supports see limited marginal gains from enhancement. Breaking this cycle requires innovation: identifying low-cost, high-impact retention strategies viable in resource-constrained settings. This dissertation addresses this gap through comparative case studies of high-need schools with unexpected retention success, identifying accessible practices that beat the odds.

Why this works:

  1. ‚úÖ Opens with clear claim (retention is a persistent challenge)
  2. ‚úÖ Supports with evidence (50% higher turnover)
  3. ‚úÖ Establishes consequences (why it matters)
  4. ‚úÖ Acknowledges what we know (factors identified)
  5. ‚úÖ Identifies what we don't know (implementation in constrained contexts)
  6. ‚úÖ Highlights the paradox (creates tension)
  7. ‚úÖ Establishes significance (cruel paradox)
  8. ‚úÖ States solution needed (innovation)
  9. ‚úÖ Connects to dissertation (how your study addresses it)
  10. ‚úÖ Uses strong, active voice throughout
  11. ‚úÖ Clusters citations efficiently
  12. ‚úÖ Tells a story (not just facts)

‚úÖ FINAL TIPS FOR SUCCESS

1. Write Multiple Drafts

  • Draft 1: Get ideas down (summaries OK here)
  • Draft 2: Organize thematically
  • Draft 3: Synthesize across studies
  • Draft 4: Add critical analysis
  • Draft 5: Polish prose and flow

2. Read Exemplar Dissertations

Find 3-5 excellent dissertations in your field:

  • Notice how they organize Chapter 2
  • Study their synthesis paragraphs
  • Note their transitional moves
  • Model your structure on what works

3. Get Feedback Early and Often

  • Share draft with chair after each major section
  • Peer review with cohort
  • Don't wait until the entire chapter is "done"
  • Early feedback prevents major revisions later

4. Use Concept Mapping

Before writing, create a concept map:

  • Major themes as main bubbles
  • Studies as smaller bubbles attached to themes
  • Lines showing relationships/contradictions
  • Write from the map (it shows your synthesis structure)

5. Remember Your Audience

You're writing for:

  • Your committee (show you know the field)
  • Future researchers (comprehensive review)
  • Practitioners (clear implications)
  • Yourself (foundation for your study)

6. Be Patient With the Process

Chapter 2 often takes 6-12 months:

  • Reading and organizing literature: 2-3 months
  • Drafting initial synthesis: 2-3 months
  • Revising based on feedback: 2-4 months
  • Final polishing: 1 month

This is normal. Quality synthesis takes time.
