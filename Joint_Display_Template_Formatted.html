Instructions:

  1. List your research questions or themes in Column 1
  2. Add quantitative findings in Column 2
  3. Add qualitative findings in Column 3
  4. Write meta-inferences (integrated conclusions) in Column 4

Tips for Effective Joint Displays

Design Principles:

  1. Keep it visual - Use tables, charts, color coding
  2. Be concise - Summarize, don't reproduce all data
  3. Show integration - Always include synthesis/meta-inference column
  4. Use clear labels - Readers should understand without explanation
  5. Be transparent - Note divergent findings, don't hide contradictions

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • ‚ùå Simply placing quant and qual side by side without integration
  • ‚ùå Making displays so complex they're unreadable
  • ‚ùå Selective reporting (only convergent findings)
  • ‚ùå Lack of interpretation/synthesis
  • ‚ùå Unclear abbreviations or coding

When to Use Each Template:

Comparison Matrix ‚Üí When you have similar themes/questions in both datasets

Case-Ordered Display ‚Üí When qualitative explains quantitative variation (especially outliers)

Instrument Development ‚Üí When you used qualitative to build quantitative measures

Theme-by-Case ‚Üí When comparing themes across groups or contexts

Statistical + Narrative ‚Üí When connecting specific statistical tests to qualitative insights

Longitudinal ‚Üí When tracking changes over time with both methods

Creating Your Own Joint Display

Step 1: Analyze each dataset separately first

  • Complete quantitative analysis
  • Complete qualitative coding and theme development

Step 2: Identify connection points

  • Where do findings address the same research question?
  • Where does one dataset explain the other?
  • Where do they diverge?

Step 3: Choose appropriate template

  • Select based on your design and purpose

Step 4: Populate the display

  • Add specific findings (not raw data)
  • Use quotes sparingly (representative examples only)
  • Keep statistical reporting concise

Step 5: Write meta-inferences

  • What do the combined findings tell you?
  • How do they answer your research question together?
  • What insights emerge from integration?

Example: Complete Joint Display in Use

Research Question: What factors influence teacher retention in high-poverty schools?

| Factor | Survey Results (n=250) | Interview Themes (n=30) | Integration |

|--------|----------------------|------------------------|-------------|

| Salary/Benefits | 3.2/5 importance rating; 45% cited as "very important" | Mixed: "Money matters but not everything," "Benefits are crucial for families" | Moderate importance. Necessary but not sufficient for retention. |

| Administrative Support | 4.6/5 importance rating; 89% cited as "very important" | Strong consensus: "Principal makes or breaks it," "Need to feel supported not blamed" | CRITICAL FACTOR. Highest rated quantitatively, strongest qualitative theme. Admin support is decisive. |

| Working Conditions | 3.8/5 importance rating; 67% cited as "very important" | "Resources matter," "Class size overwhelming," "Facilities deteriorating" | Important factor. Infrastructure and resources affect daily experience. |

| Career Growth | 3.5/5 importance rating; 58% cited as "very important" | Divided: Veterans less concerned, early-career teachers want "path to leadership" | Moderate importance, varies by career stage. Critical for early-career retention. |

Meta-Inference: Administrative support emerges as the most critical retention factor, with both quantitative ratings and qualitative emphasis converging. While salary is important, it's a threshold factor‚Äîsufficient compensation is necessary but strong leadership is decisive. Working conditions and career growth opportunities show moderate importance, with some variation by teacher characteristics.

Implications: Retention interventions should prioritize leadership development and administrative support over salary increases alone.
